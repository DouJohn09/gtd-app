What Is Getting Things Done (GTD)? A Beginner's Guide

June 29, 2026 · 8 min read · by the Cleartable team

If you've spent any time looking for a way to feel less scattered, you've probably bumped into three letters: GTD. It stands for Getting Things Done, a productivity method introduced by David Allen in his 2001 book of the same name, and it's still the most influential system of its kind two decades on. But the name can be misleading — GTD isn't really about doing more. It's about getting everything off your mind so you can think clearly and act with calm.

This guide is a plain-English introduction: what GTD actually is, the five steps it runs on, the idea behind it, and how to start without turning it into a second job. No jargon, no system worship — just the parts that genuinely help.

The core idea: your mind is for having ideas, not holding them

The insight at the heart of GTD is that your brain is a terrible filing cabinet. Every unfinished task you try to "just remember" — call the dentist, reply to that email, fix the thing — sits in the back of your mind using up attention, nagging at random moments, and never quite letting you relax. Allen calls these "open loops." The more of them you carry, the more frazzled you feel, regardless of how much you're actually doing.

GTD's solution is to move every open loop out of your head and into a trusted external system — one you review often enough to genuinely believe nothing will fall through the cracks. Once your mind trusts that system, it can stop guarding all those loose threads and give its full attention to whatever is in front of you. Allen calls this state "mind like water": calm, responsive, and unbothered until something actually needs you.

The five steps of GTD

GTD turns that idea into a repeatable workflow with five stages. You don't do all five constantly — you capture all day, clarify and organize regularly, reflect weekly, and engage whenever you sit down to work.

1. Capture

Collect everything that has your attention into a small number of trusted "inboxes" — a notes app, a paper pad, your email inbox. Tasks, ideas, errands, worries, half-thoughts: if it's pulling at you, it goes in. The point isn't to organize yet; it's just to get it out of your head. A brain dump is exactly this step done in one sitting, and it's the fastest way to feel the relief GTD promises.

2. Clarify

Now process what you captured, one item at a time, and decide what each thing is. Ask: is it actionable? If not, trash it, file it as reference, or park it as "someday/maybe." If it is actionable, decide the very next physical action. If it takes less than two minutes, do it right then — Allen's famous "two-minute rule." If it's bigger than a single action, it's a project, and it needs its own outcome and next step. Clarifying is where vague stuff ("Mom's birthday") becomes a concrete action ("book a table for Saturday").

3. Organize

Put each clarified item where it belongs so you can find it at the right moment. Next actions go on lists — often grouped by context, like @calls, @errands, or @computer, so you can batch similar work. Things that must happen on a specific day go on your calendar (and only those — the calendar is sacred, not a wish list). Projects get a list of their own. Reference material gets filed away. The goal is simple: nothing lives in your head, and everything has a home.

4. Reflect

A system you don't review is a system you won't trust — and an untrusted system sends everything right back into your head. So GTD asks you to look over your lists regularly, and once a week to do a proper weekly review: clear your inboxes, update your project and action lists, and look at the week ahead. This is the keystone habit of the whole method. Skip it and GTD quietly falls apart; keep it and the rest mostly takes care of itself.

5. Engage

Finally, you actually do the work — and because the first four steps did their job, this part gets much simpler. Instead of agonizing over the whole mountain, you look at your next-action lists and pick what to do based on your context, time, energy, and priority. When you've already decided what matters most, the choice of what to do next is calm rather than frantic. Engaging is the payoff for the trust you built in the other four steps.

What GTD gets right — and where people get stuck

GTD's lasting strength is that it's tool-agnostic and psychologically honest. It doesn't promise motivation or hustle; it just removes the mental drag of unfinished business, which is a real and underrated source of stress. The "capture everything" and "next physical action" ideas alone are worth adopting even if you never touch the rest.

Where people get stuck is in over-building it. The contexts, lists, and reviews can become an elaborate hobby that's more fun to maintain than the actual work — and a system that takes an hour to tend every day defeats its own purpose. The other classic failure is skipping the weekly review: without it, your lists drift out of date, you stop trusting them, and within a few weeks you're back to carrying everything in your head. Start light, and only add structure when you feel a real need for it.

How to start GTD this week

You don't need to read the book or buy anything to begin. Try this:

  • Do one big capture. Spend 20 minutes writing down every open loop you can think of, work and personal.
  • Clarify the list. For each item, decide the next action, do the two-minute ones immediately, and mark which items are really projects.
  • Pick one trusted home. Move your actions into a single place you'll actually check — not five scattered apps and sticky notes.
  • Book your first weekly review. Put 30–45 minutes on the calendar for the end of the week. This one habit is what makes it stick.

That's enough to feel the core benefit. You can layer in contexts and someday/maybe lists later, once the basics are a habit.

The bottom line

Getting Things Done isn't about cramming more into your day — it's about getting everything out of your head and into a system you trust, so your attention is free for the work itself. Capture it all, clarify the next action, organize it into a home, review it weekly, and then simply engage. Start small, protect the weekly review, and resist the urge to over-engineer. Done lightly, GTD delivers the one thing most productivity advice can't: a quieter mind.

Frequently asked questions

What is Getting Things Done (GTD) in simple terms?

Getting Things Done, or GTD, is a productivity method created by David Allen. Its core idea is simple: get everything out of your head and into a trusted system, then process it into clear next actions, so your mind is free to focus on doing the work instead of remembering it. It runs on five steps — capture, clarify, organize, reflect, and engage — and works for any tool, paper or digital.

What are the five steps of GTD?

The five steps are: Capture (collect everything that has your attention into one place), Clarify (decide what each item is and what action it needs), Organize (put each item where it belongs — a project, a next-action list, a calendar, or someday/maybe), Reflect (review your lists regularly, especially in a weekly review, so you trust them), and Engage (simply do the next actions with a clear mind). The first four make the system trustworthy; the fifth is where the work actually happens.

Do I need special software to do GTD?

No. GTD is a method, not an app — David Allen designed it to work with a notebook and folders if you like. What matters is having one trusted place to capture things, a habit of clarifying them into next actions, and a regular review. A good task app simply lowers the friction: it captures quickly, keeps your lists organized, and reminds you to review, so the habit is easier to keep than it would be on paper.
