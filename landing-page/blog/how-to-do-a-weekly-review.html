How to Do a Weekly Review: A Step-by-Step Guide

June 17, 2026 · 7 min read · by the Cleartable team

Most task systems fail for the same quiet reason: not because they lack features, but because you stop trusting them. A few stale projects, an inbox you haven't emptied in two weeks, a handful of "I'll remember that" items that never made it onto a list — and suddenly the app feels like a museum of good intentions rather than a tool you actually rely on.

The weekly review is the habit that fixes this. It's the single most important ritual in Getting Things Done, the productivity method created by David Allen, and it's what separates people who keep a calm, trusted system from people who restart their productivity setup every few months. This guide walks through exactly how to do one.

What is a weekly review?

A weekly review is a recurring appointment with yourself — usually 30 to 90 minutes once a week — where you process everything that's accumulated, bring all your lists up to date, and look ahead at what's coming. The goal isn't to do the work. It's to get your system back to a state you can trust, so that during the rest of the week you can focus on execution instead of worrying about what you might be forgetting.

It helps to break the review into three moves: clear your inboxes, update your lists, and look ahead. (This mirrors the three phases David Allen describes in Getting Things Done.) Let's go through each.

Step 1 — Clear your inboxes

The first move is emptying every place where "stuff" collects so nothing is left uncaptured. Loose items in your head are what create the low-grade anxiety that the rest of the system is meant to remove.

  • Collect loose papers and notes. Receipts, sticky notes, business cards, scraps. Get them into your inbox.
  • Empty your digital inboxes. Email, messages, your notes app, the screenshots folder — anything actionable becomes a task; anything reference gets filed; anything useless gets deleted.
  • Empty your task inbox. Every captured item should be clarified: is it actionable? If yes, what's the very next physical action? If no, is it trash, reference, or someday/maybe?
  • Do a mind sweep. Spend five minutes writing down anything still rattling around in your head — commitments, worries, ideas — and drop them into your inbox to process.

Step 2 — Update your lists

Now that nothing is uncaptured, make your existing lists reflect reality. This is the part most people skip, and it's exactly why their systems drift out of date.

  • Review your Next Actions list. Tick off anything already done, delete what's no longer relevant, and add any new next actions.
  • Review past calendar. Look back at the last week or two for follow-ups, things that triggered new actions, or items you said you'd do.
  • Review upcoming calendar. Look ahead a few weeks. What needs preparation? Block time now for anything that does.
  • Review your Waiting For list. What are you blocked on? Who owes you something? Nudge anyone who's gone quiet.
  • Review your Projects. For each active project, confirm it has at least one clear next action. A project with no next action is a stalled project — and stalled projects are how systems quietly rot.

Step 3 — Look ahead

With everything captured and your lists current, your mind has the space to think bigger. This is the most enjoyable phase and the one that turns a maintenance chore into something genuinely valuable.

  • Review your Someday/Maybe list. Is anything ready to become an active project? Move it. Is anything no longer interesting? Drop it.
  • Capture new ideas. Now that you're not anxious about loose ends, write down anything you'd like to explore, learn, or build.

A weekly review checklist

Run through these in order. The whole thing should take under an hour once you're in the rhythm.

  1. Collect loose papers and notes into your inbox
  2. Empty your email and messaging inboxes
  3. Process your task inbox to zero
  4. Do a five-minute mind sweep
  5. Review and update your Next Actions
  6. Review the past two weeks of your calendar
  7. Review the next few weeks of your calendar
  8. Review your Waiting For list
  9. Confirm every active project has a next action
  10. Review Someday/Maybe and capture new ideas

Common weekly review mistakes

Doing work instead of reviewing

The review is for deciding, not doing. If you find yourself answering emails or knocking out tasks, you'll never finish the review. Note the action and move on.

Skipping it when you're busy

The weeks you feel too busy to review are exactly the weeks you need it most. A skipped review is how backlogs build and trust erodes. Protect the slot.

Making it too elaborate

A review you actually do beats a perfect one you dread. Start with the checklist above and adjust over time.

The bottom line

A task system is only as trustworthy as it is current, and the weekly review is what keeps it current. Block a consistent slot, run the checklist, and you'll spend the rest of your week executing with a clear head instead of wondering what you've forgotten. It's the highest-leverage hour in a productivity practice — and once it's a habit, you'll never want to go without it.

Frequently asked questions

How long should a weekly review take?

Most people complete a weekly review in 30 to 90 minutes. When you first start it can run longer because your system has a backlog. Once it becomes a habit, an hour is typical and many people get it down to 30 minutes.

When is the best time to do a weekly review?

Pick a consistent slot you can protect every week. Friday afternoon is popular because it closes the work week and lets you start Monday clear, but Sunday evening or Monday morning work just as well. The day matters less than doing it on the same day every week.

What are the three steps of a weekly review?

It helps to break a weekly review into three moves: clear your inboxes so nothing is left uncaptured, update your lists and projects so they reflect reality, and look ahead at your calendar and someday/maybe list. This mirrors the three phases David Allen describes in Getting Things Done.
